Brutally Honest Architecture Critique: Gold Trading Confidence
System
TL;DR — The Brutal Truth
Your system is a classic case of “complexity theater” — 28 files, 6 layers, 2 RL agents, 7 quant
models, 3 AI services, and a fusion engine, all trained on free Yahoo Finance 1-minute bars with
mock data fallbacks, producing confidence scores that will almost certainly have no correlation
with actual trade profitability out-of-sample. The Dueling DQN and Sequence CNN agents are
statistically doomed to overfit on a single non-stationary timeseries. The Heston, SABR, and Local
Vol models are theoretically miscalibrated because you’re fitting them to OHLC bars rather than
option prices. The FinBERT sentiment pipeline adds noise, not signal, at the 1-minute timeframe. The
precision system’s meta-labeler is circularly validated. The entire signal fusion has no theoretical
foundation for why combining these specific signals should produce better confidence than any single one.
You are building a very impressive-looking Rube Goldberg machine that will produce beautifully
calibrated backtests and catastrophic live results. The single biggest issue: you’re optimizing for
Sharpe ratio in a backtest, which is mathematically equivalent to optimizing for historical
curve-fit [14 ].

1. The Fundamental Paradox: Confidence Scoring Without Ground Truth
1.1 The Core Logical Flaw
Your entire product promises to tell users a “confidence score” about trades. But confidence in what? The
system computes a percentage (0-100%) representing how confident it is that a trade will be profitable.
Here’s the devastating problem: there is no way to validate this confidence score in real-time.
A weather app can say “70% chance of rain” and you can count raindrops. Your system says “85%
confidence this gold trade will win” — but the outcome (profit/loss) won’t be known until hours or days
later. More critically, the feedback loop for improving confidence calibration is extraordinarily slow
— you need hundreds of trades at each confidence level to determine if 85% confidence actually produces
85% win rates. With 1-minute gold trading, you might get 20-50 trades per day, meaning you need weeks
of live trading data just to validate one confidence decile [8 ].
1.2 The Self-Fulfilling Prophecy Problem
Your precision system uses XGBoost with isotonic calibration to produce confidence scores. Isotonic
calibration requires known outcomes to learn the mapping from raw scores to probabilities. In training,
you have these (historical labels). In production, you don’t. The calibration will drift immediately
as market conditions change, and you won’t know it’s drifted until you’ve accumulated enough post-hoc
trade outcomes to measure it — by which time your users have already acted on miscalibrated signals
[57 ]. This is the quant equivalent of selling a scale that calibrates itself based on objects whose weight
you’ll only know tomorrow.

2. Reinforcement Learning: The Siren Song
2.1 Why DQN for Trading Is Fundamentally Problematic
Your architecture features both a Dueling Double DQN (single-bar) and a Sequence CNN MultiHead DQN (30-bar history). Both use discrete action spaces: BUY, SELL, HOLD. Academic literature
and practitioner experience consistently show that DQN-based trading agents face insurmountable
structural problems [22 ][23 ]:
Discrete actions on continuous prices is a category error. Trading involves continuous decisions:
how much to buy, at what price limit, with what stop-loss. Reducing this to three discrete actions throws
away the most important degrees of freedom. A DQN that outputs “BUY” gives you no information
about position size, entry price, or risk management — the very things your precision system later tries
to reconstruct [25 ].
The Q-value overestimation problem is well-documented in DQN literature. Even with Double DQN
mitigation, Q-values tend to be optimistically biased in stochastic environments. Financial markets are
maximally stochastic — the Q-value for “BUY” at a given state is essentially the expected return of
a random variable with enormous variance. The DQN learns to chase spurious correlations because the
signal-to-noise ratio in 1-minute gold returns is effectively zero [4 ][36 ].
2.2 The Catastrophic Overfitting Reality
Your training pipeline uses “supervised pre-training as a 3-class classifier before DQN fine-tuning.” This is
exactly the approach that research has shown fails systematically [29 ][30 ]. The supervised phase teaches
the network to memorize historical patterns (overfit). The DQN phase then fine-tunes these memorized
patterns toward reward optimization — but the patterns were never real to begin with. The result: an
agent that appears to learn sophisticated trading behavior but is actually exploiting historical noise
[6 ].
Industry research demonstrates that even state-of-the-art DRL agents with far more sophisticated architectures than yours (PPO, A3C, DDPG) struggle to beat simple buy-and-hold benchmarks
once realistic transaction costs are applied [14 ][21 ]. Your $0.30 spread + $0.10 slippage assumption (total
$0.40 per round-trip) sounds reasonable until you realize that on 1-minute gold bars, the typical price
movement between consecutive bars is often less than $0.40. You’re trying to predict signal in
an environment where transaction costs exceed the typical signal magnitude [48 ].
2.3 Pre-Training Then Fine-Tuning = Catastrophic Forgetting
Recent research on combining supervised fine-tuning (SFT) with reinforcement learning shows a critical
phenomenon: SFT forgets RL knowledge, and RL forgets SFT knowledge [29 ]. Your pipeline
explicitly does SFT first (CE loss) then DQN fine-tuning. The DQN phase will overwrite the supervisedlearned patterns, and not in a good way — it will overwrite them with reward-hacking behaviors that
exploit backtest-specific artifacts. The 2025 paper “Mitigating Forgetting Between Supervised and Reinforcement Learning” from Georgia Tech and Adobe Research shows that this forgetting is so severe
that specialized techniques (parameter freezing, entropy-based token selection) are needed to prevent it
— techniques your system doesn’t implement [29 ][33 ].
2.4 Why Sequence CNN Won’t Save You
The Sequence CNN agent processes 30 bars of history through 1D convolutions. This is architecturally
reasonable for temporal pattern detection. However, the fundamental problem remains: there are no

3. Data Quality: The Foundation of Sand
3.1 Yahoo Finance 1-Minute Bars: Unusable for Serious Trading
Your primary data source is Yahoo Finance 1-minute bars for GC=F. This is the single most damaging
choice in your entire architecture. Here’s what the research and practitioner community knows about
free 1-minute data [24 ]:
Missing bars are endemic. A study of 143 stocks found that over 60% had missing 1-minute
data points. For a 14-session period expecting 5,250 data points per stock, some had fewer than 4,400
— missing over 16% of bars. Your system’s “microstructure filter” and “Kalman smoother” can’t
reconstruct data that was never recorded [24 ].
OHLC bars destroy microstructure information. Your precision system claims to compute VPIN,
CVD, volume profile, and microstructure features — but these require tick-level data (individual trades
and quotes). 1-minute OHLC bars aggregate hundreds or thousands of individual trades into four numbers, irreversibly destroying the very information your microstructure layer claims to extract. Computing
“Roll’s effective spread” from OHLC bars is statistically meaningless — Roll’s estimator requires sequential trade prices, not bar summaries [40 ].
Survivorship bias and stale quotes. Yahoo Finance data includes periods where the exchange was
closed, quotes were stale, or data feeds had issues. Your mock data fallback (generating synthetic data
with controlled patterns) is academic malpractice — training on synthetic patterns guarantees the
model will find those patterns, but they won’t exist in reality.
3.2 The Mock Data Fallback Is Worse Than No Data
Your market_data.py includes a generate_mock_gold_data function that creates synthetic data with “realistic gold patterns.” This is poison for your system:
• The mock data has controlled patterns (trend, mean-reversion, session effects)
• The RL agents and precision system will learn these artificial patterns
• In production, when Yahoo Finance fails, the system falls back to data that its own models
were trained on
• This creates a self-fulfilling hallucination where the system “predicts” patterns it generated

3.3 Feature Engineering on Noisy Data = Amplified Noise
Your system extracts 21 static features + 4 dynamic features from 1-minute bars. Technical indicators
(RSI, MACD, EMA, Bollinger) computed on low-quality 1-minute data are mathematically unstable.
A small gap in data or a single spurious tick can flip an RSI from oversold to overbought. Your Kalman
smoother helps, but it can’t create information that isn’t there — it can only smooth over gaps,
making them invisible but still wrong [43 ].

4. Quant Models: Theoretical Overkill, Practical Underkill
4.1 Heston Model: Calibrated to What?
You implement a full Heston stochastic volatility model with mean-reversion, vol-of-vol, and correlation
parameters. This is a beautiful mathematical object — for option pricing. The Heston model is
designed to price European options by fitting its 5 parameters to observed implied volatility surfaces.
You’re using it on historical gold price bars without any option data. This is like using a Formula 1
engine to power a bicycle [38 ][39 ].
The Heston parameters (kappa, theta, sigma, rho, v0) are notoriously unstable when calibrated to daily
data — research shows only 45.8% of daily calibrations produce statistically significant parameter
estimates [41 ]. On 1-minute bars, the parameters will swing wildly and produce meaningless forecasts.
The “volatility of volatility” parameter becomes a random number generator.
4.2 SABR and Local Volatility: Wrong Tool for the Job
SABR (Stochastic Alpha Beta Rho) and Local Volatility (Dupire) models are option pricing models,
not time-series forecasting tools. They describe how implied volatility varies with strike price — information you don’t have because you’re not using option data. Running these models on spot price history
produces numbers that are mathematically valid but economically meaningless [44 ].
4.3 GARCH: The Right Idea, Wrong Application
GARCH (1,1) for volatility forecasting is theoretically sound, but your implementation has critical issues.
GARCH models assume stationary volatility regimes — but gold volatility shifts dramatically around
Fed announcements, geopolitical events, and market crises [53 ][55 ]. A GARCH model fit on calm-period
data will catastrophically underestimate volatility during crisis periods, exactly when you need
accurate risk forecasts most.
Research shows GARCH-type models “struggle with rapid market structural changes” and “when a new
small sample emerges, indicating a sharp drop or surge, the model assimilates it within the entire data
set” — meaning it responds too slowly to regime changes [55 ].
4.4 Monte Carlo Simulation: Garbage In, Garbage Out
Your Monte Carlo engine simulates 200 paths of Geometric Brownian Motion for 30 days. The problem:
GBM is a terrible model for gold prices. Gold exhibits:
• Volatility clustering (not captured by constant-vol GBM)
• Jump discontinuities (not captured by continuous-path GBM)
• Negative autocorrelation in returns at short horizons (not captured by GBM)

4.5 Black-Scholes on Gold: Category Error
Black-Scholes assumes constant volatility, no jumps, log-normal returns, and frictionless markets. Gold

5. Sentiment Analysis: Noise at 1-Minute Resolution
5.1 FinBERT: The Wrong Model for the Wrong Data
Your sentiment pipeline uses FinBERT (DistilRoBERTa) on news headlines, then feeds sentiment scores
into the confidence fusion. Research on FinBERT for trading reveals consistent problems [49 ][50 ]:
FinBERT performs worse than DistilRoBERTa on social media data. A comprehensive study
found that “FinBERT demonstrated worse results than DistilRoBERTa” across Twitter and Reddit
datasets, achieving only marginally better performance on formal news articles. On noisy social media text, FinBERT’s financial specialization actually hurts generalization [49 ].
Sentiment-to-price correlation is weak and lagged. Research shows “medium correlation between
sentiment scores and stock prices” at best, with the relationship being “multifactorial” — sentiment
explains only a tiny fraction of price variance. At the 1-minute timeframe, news sentiment is essentially
uncorrelated with price movements because:
• News breaks at discrete times, not continuously
• Market reaction to news happens in seconds, not minutes
• By the time your RSS aggregator fetches, FinBERT processes, and the fusion engine incorporates
sentiment, the price has already moved
• Most “news” is irrelevant price commentary, not causal information [^47^]
5.2 DeepSeek LLM for Trading: Unproven and Unreliable
Your system uses DeepSeek LLM API as a secondary sentiment source. Academic benchmarks show LLM
agents for trading have massive variance in performance — “DeepSeek-V3 exhibited the smallest
performance fluctuation” but this was in a specific controlled benchmark, and even the best LLM trading
agents “rarely outperform simple baselines” [63 ]. The Alpha Arena live trading experiment showed
DeepSeek initially leading with 11% returns but then crashing alongside all other models when market
conditions changed [59 ].
LLMs suffer from training data leakage — they may have seen financial news from your test period
during their training, making their “predictions” actually post-hoc reconstructions. Your “8-second timeout with keyword fallback” means that under load, the system falls back to a keyword algorithm that
has zero predictive validity [56 ].

6. Precision Trading System: Layered Complexity, Layered Problems
6.1 Microstructure Features on Aggregated Data
Your Layer 1 computes “smart price,” Kalman-smoothed estimates, and Roll spread from 1-minute OHLC
bars. This is statistically invalid:
• Smart price requires bid-ask midpoints from quote data
• Kalman smoothing on 4 data points per minute can’t separate signal from noise
• Roll’s estimator requires sequential transaction prices, not bar summaries
• The “spread filter” threshold of $1.00 for gold is arbitrary and regime-dependent
These features give the illusion of sophistication while processing data too aggregated to support them
[40 ].
6.2 FVG Detection on 1-Minute Gold
Fair Value Gap detection (Layer 2) is borrowed from Smart Money Concepts, which operates on 5-minute
to daily charts with clean price action. On 1-minute gold:
• FVGs form and fill constantly due to noise
• The “80% retracement without filling” statistic has no empirical basis
• Gold’s tendency to wick-stop-hunt makes FVG-based entries extremely high false-positive

• VPIN requires tick-level trade classification (buyer-initiated vs seller-initiated)
• Your 1-minute bars don’t have this information
• CVD requires the same trade-by-trade classification
• The “absorption” signal you claim to detect requires order book depth data, which you don’t

6.4 XGBoost Meta-Labeler: Circular Validation
Your Layer 5 uses XGBoost to predict whether a trade will be profitable, with isotonic calibration. This

6.5 Quarter-Kelly Sizing: Dangerous in Practice
Your Layer 6 uses quarter-Kelly position sizing. Kelly criterion requires knowing your true win rate and
reward-to-risk ratio with high precision. Research shows:
• A 5-percentage-point error in win rate estimate changes Kelly fraction by 3x [^57^]
• Full Kelly produces 50% drawdowns with ~1/3 probability [^57^][^58^]
• Quarter-Kelly is safer but still assumes known, stationary edge statistics
• Your edge estimates come from backtests, which overestimate true edge by 2-5x due to overfitting
• In practice, your “quarter-Kelly” is probably closer to full-Kelly on an overestimated edge,
meaning users risk 25-50% drawdowns [^60^][^62^]

7. Backtesting: The Illusion of Validation
7.1 Walk-Forward Analysis Won’t Save You
Your system implements walk-forward cross-validation with embargo periods — this is good practice.
However, walk-forward has critical limitations that your architecture doesn’t address [14 ][15 ][18 ]:
Window selection bias: The choice of in-sample vs out-of-sample window sizes “is never entirely
objective.” Your 5:1 ratio (train:test) is standard, but the results are sensitive to this choice, and you
have no theoretical basis for selecting it [15 ].
Non-stationary markets: Walk-forward “responds to regime changes with a lag.” When gold transitions from calm to crisis (e.g., a geopolitical shock), your system will continue trading on parameters
optimized for the old regime until the next retraining window — exactly when losses are most severe
[18 ].
The multiple testing problem: Your system optimizes across DQN hyperparameters, CNN architectures, XGBoost parameters, precision layer thresholds, and fusion weights. With even modest parameter

7.2 The Sharpe Ratio Is the Wrong Metric
Your system optimizes for Sharpe ratio. Academic research demonstrates that:
• Sharpe ratio is notoriously gameable — strategies can optimize it while producing poor live
returns
• The deflated Sharpe ratio correction is necessary but insuﬀicient — it doesn’t account for all forms
of selection bias
• Sortino ratio (which you also compute) is better but still backward-looking
• Maximum drawdown and Calmar ratio are more meaningful for user experience, but your system
doesn’t optimize for them [^14^][^16^]
7.3 Transaction Cost Assumptions Are Optimistic
Your backtest uses $0.30 spread + $0.10 slippage per leg ($0.40 total per round-trip). For 1-minute gold
trading, this is aggressively optimistic:
• Retail gold spreads during Asian session: $0.50-$2.00
• During NFP/FOMC releases: $2.00-$5.00+
• Slippage on market orders in fast-moving gold: $0.10-$0.50 per leg
• Commission on top (typically $3-7 per lot round-trip at ECN brokers) [^48^][^32^]
Your costs assume optimal execution conditions. Live trading will cost 2-5x more, turning slightly
profitable backtests into guaranteed losses.

11. Recommendations: What To Do Instead
11.1 Immediate Actions (Do These First)
1. Replace Yahoo Finance with quality data. Minimum: Polygon.io or TickData for 1-minute
bars. Ideal: tick-level data from a professional vendor. Without this, nothing else matters.
2. Remove the mock data fallback. If the data source fails, the system should stop trading, not
hallucinate patterns.
3. Simplify the RL agents. Replace Dueling DQN + Sequence CNN with a single PPO agent
operating on hourly bars with continuous action outputs for position sizing. Or better: remove
RL entirely and use a simple rule-based system until you have proven edge.
4. Remove the quant models (Heston, SABR, Local Vol, Monte Carlo) or restrict them to actual
option pricing if you add options data. They add no value for directional spot trading.
11.2 Medium-Term Restructuring
5. Drop to a longer timeframe. 1-minute gold trading is a negative-sum game after costs for
retail traders. Move to 15-minute or hourly signals. The “edge per trade” increases while cost-pertrade stays constant, making profitability theoretically possible.
Generated by Kimi.ai

6. Remove sentiment analysis or relegate it to a weekly macro context overlay, not 1-minute
signal fusion. News sentiment has zero predictive power at intraday frequencies.
7. Replace the 6-layer precision system with a single XGBoost model on a small set of carefully
engineered features, validated with purged cross-validation (Lopez de Prado’s method). Simpler
models generalize better.
8. Implement proper cost modeling. Use variable spreads ($0.30 in London session, $1.50+ in
Asian), realistic slippage ($0.10-$0.50), and commission ($3-7/lot).
11.3 Strategic Reorientation
9. Stop building confidence scores and start building a trade journal. The most valuable
product might be post-trade analysis (what went wrong, what patterns preceded wins/losses)
rather than pre-trade prediction (which is likely impossible).
10. Pivot from prediction to risk management. If you can’t predict direction (you almost certainly
can’t), focus on what you can control: position sizing, stop-loss optimization, and portfolio heat
management. A system that helps users not lose money is more valuable than one that claims
to predict the future.
11. Run a paper trading competition. Put your system against simple benchmarks (random entry,
buy-and-hold, EMA crossover) for 6 months minimum. If it can’t beat these with statistical
significance, it has no edge.

12. The Bottom Line
Your system represents hundreds of hours of sophisticated engineering applied to a fundamentally flawed premise: that 1-minute gold price movements are predictable using retail-grade data
and academic quant models. The architecture diagram is beautiful. The code is well-structured. The
academic references are appropriate. But the core thesis is wrong.
The evidence from academic research, practitioner experience, and basic statistical theory all points to
the same conclusion: retail 1-minute gold prediction is not a viable machine learning problem.
The signal-to-noise ratio is too low, the costs are too high, the data quality is too poor, and the market
microstructure is too adversarial.
The good news: you have a talented engineering team that can build complex systems. Redirect that
talent toward problems that are actually solvable: longer-timeframe trend following, multi-asset
portfolio allocation, risk management tools, or post-trade analytics. The world doesn’t need another
overfitted gold prediction bot. It needs tools that help traders survive — because survival, compounded
over years, is the only real edge.
